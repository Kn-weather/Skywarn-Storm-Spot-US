Spotter Safety

As a spotter, your personal safety is the primary objective. Review and follow these guidelines before every deployment.

Core Safety Guidelines

  1. The spotter's personal safety is the primary objective of every spotter.
  2. The spotter should adhere to the concept of ACES at all times.
  3. The spotter should obey federal, state, and local laws and directives from public safety officials.
  4. The spotter should never put themselves in harm's way. This includes attempting to walk or drive over obstructions (e.g., flooded roadways and downed power lines) and positioning themselves under objects that have a potential to fall or be blown over due to severe weather.

The ACES Concept

The ACES concept, commonly used by emergency management personnel, stands for Awareness, Communication, Escape Routes, and Safe Zones. If you remember ACES, you can remain safe in any situation, including spotting.

A
Awareness

Constantly observe the situation around you — sometimes called situational awareness. Continuously monitoring risks can save your life, especially in rapidly changing weather conditions. Knowing nearby river crossings, power poles, or trees helps you prepare for hazards. When aware of imminent threats and thinking ahead, you can position yourself to minimize risk.

C
Communication

Communicating your whereabouts to others on a regular basis and having multiple lines of communication available can keep you and others safe from hazards. Always let someone know where you are and where you are going.

E
Escape Routes

Escape routes are important when you need to get away from danger. An escape route is a clear path that allows you to reach your safe zone well before harm arrives. Anytime you are acting as a spotter, continually note and monitor escape routes available to you. A proper escape route always leads to a safe zone.

S
Safe Zones

A safe zone or shelter is an area in which you are safe during a hazardous condition. Your safe zone can be a location to shelter in place or an area completely away from the hazardous event. In severe weather, your safe zone could be the lowest portion of a sturdy building away from all windows. For mobile spotting, it could be an area away from the severe weather. Always clearly identify and continually monitor your safe zone.

Remembering ACES — remain aware of your surroundings, have open lines of communication, know your escape routes, and know your safe zones — wherever you go can increase your safety.

Soundings Skew-T / Log-P

Click the map to fetch the nearest RAOB observed sounding, or pick a station from the dropdown. Soundings update at 00Z and 12Z daily.

Skew-T Legend & How to Read

Chart Lines

Isobars — Horizontal lines of equal pressure (hPa). Log-spaced: 1000 hPa at bottom (sea level) to 100 hPa at top (stratosphere). Labeled on the left.
Isotherms — Skewed lines of equal temperature (°C). Slant from bottom-left to top-right. Labeled at the bottom. 0°C highlighted white.
Dry Adiabats — Solid brown-red curves sloping SE to NW. Show how unsaturated air cools as it rises (~9.8°C/km). Lines of constant potential temperature (θ).
Moist Adiabats — Solid green-cyan curves. Show how saturated air cools as it rises (~4°C/km near surface, approaches dry rate aloft). Lines of constant equivalent potential temperature (θe).
Mixing Ratio Lines — Dashed green lines. Lines of constant water vapor content (g/kg). Slant from bottom-left to top-right, nearly parallel to isotherms.
Temperature Trace — Color-coded by altitude: red (low-level <700hPa), orange (mid 700-500hPa), yellow (high 500-300hPa), cyan (upper >300hPa). The actual measured environmental temperature.
Dewpoint Trace — Solid green line. Always left of (colder than) the temperature trace. Shows moisture content; closer to temp trace = more humid.
Parcel Ascent — Dashed yellow line. Shows the path a surface parcel would take if lifted. Used to calculate CAPE, CIN, LI.
Parcel Levels: ●LCL (Lifted Condensation Level — cloud base) · ●LFC (Level of Free Convection — where parcel becomes buoyant) · ●EL (Equilibrium Level — where buoyancy ends)

How to Read the Chart

Y-Axis (Pressure): Higher pressure = lower altitude (bottom). Lower pressure = higher altitude (top). The log spacing compresses upper levels — this is normal and matches how the atmosphere thins with height.
X-Axis (Temperature): Skewed at 45°. Cold temperatures are on the left, warm on the right. The skew makes the adiabats appear as gentle curves instead of steep lines.
Instability (CAPE): The area between the parcel ascent (yellow dashed) and the temperature trace (red), from LFC to EL, where the parcel is warmer than the environment. Larger area = more CAPE = stronger updrafts.
Capping (CIN): The negative area below the LFC where the parcel is colder than the environment. Strong cap = storms won't form unless broken. Weak cap = storms form easily but may be disorganized.
Moisture: The gap between the temperature (red) and dewpoint (green) traces shows how dry the air is. Wide gap = dry air. Narrow gap = humid. A dewpoint that drops sharply with height indicates a dry layer (important for severe weather — evaporative cooling, downbursts).
Wind Shear: The wind barbs on the right side show speed and direction at each level. Large changes in direction/speed between levels = strong shear. Check the hodograph (below) for a visual representation.
0°C Freezing Level: The white dashed isotherm. Precipitation type (rain vs. snow vs. freezing rain) depends on where this sits relative to the surface and any warm layers aloft.

Hodograph

0-1 km (red) — Low-level shear. Long segment = strong low-level shear = tornado potential.
1-3 km (orange) — Mid-level shear.
3-6 km (yellow) — Deep-layer shear. Long segment = supercell potential.
6-12 km (green) — Upper-level flow.
Storm Motion (RM) — Cyan dashed arrow. Bunkers right-mover supercell motion vector.
Shape: Straight line = unidirectional shear. Clockwise curve (veering) = warm advection, tornado-favorable. Counter-clockwise (backing) = cold advection.

Key Parameters (right panel)

CAPE: >500 (green) · >1500 (amber) · >2500 (red) J/kg. Convective available potential energy — fuel for updrafts.
Lifted Index (LI): <0 (green) · <-4 (amber) · <-6 (red). Negative = unstable. More negative = more unstable.
Bulk Shear: >25kt (amber) · >40kt (red). 0-6km shear >40kt supports organized severe storms/supercells.
SRH (Storm-Relative Helicity): >100 (amber) · >200 (red) m²/s². 0-1km SRH >100 supports tornadoes; >200 = significant tornado potential.
Precipitable Water (PW): Total moisture in the atmospheric column. >35mm = heavy rain/flood threat. >50mm = extreme moisture (atmospheric river).
References: NOAA JetStream · NWS ZHU Training
